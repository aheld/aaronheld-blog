# Contrarian View: If Erling Haaland Were a Software Engineer, He'd Never Get Promoted

## Strongest argument against the premise

**Football has a goal metric. Software doesn't. That asymmetry is the whole problem, and the analogy quietly skips it.**

Haaland survives his flat activity dashboard because football has one perfectly unambiguous outcome measure: goals. Everyone agrees what a goal is, who scored it, and that it wins matches. Nobody needs proxies for Haaland because the outcome is directly observable, instantly attributable, and updated in real time.

Software has no goals column. Outcomes are diffuse (whose work made the quarter?), lagged (the architecture pays off in year three), and confounded (the system was stable, but was that design or luck or low traffic?). Managers don't count commits because they're stupid; they count commits because the thing they actually want to count doesn't exist as a number. Tell a manager "just look at outcomes, like football does" and you have told them nothing actionable. The absence of chaos is also exactly what coasting looks like. A flat dashboard is consistent with a quiet genius, a checked-out senior, and an over-engineer gold-plating a system nobody uses. Distinguishing them takes deep technical judgment that promotion committees at scale usually don't have, which is *why* legibility (visibility, artifacts, war-room appearances) became the currency in the first place. Visibility isn't vanity. In a large organization, it's how trust propagates past the people who can directly evaluate your work.

A skeptical reader will also note the team-composition problem: Haaland only affords to walk because ten teammates run. Manchester City is a machine built to deliver him the ball in the box; De Bruyne and Rodri do the "Kane work." A team of eleven Haalands loses every match. If your promotion system starts optimizing for quiet finishers, who builds the platform, reviews the designs, and answers the pages?

## Credible voices on the other side

- **Roy Keane** (Sky Sports / Stick to Football, April–May 2024): "In front of goal he's the best in the world, but his general play... is so poor. He's almost like a League Two player." ([SportBible](https://www.sportbible.com/football/football-news/man-city/man-city-erling-haaland-roy-keane-league-two-228255-20240510), [Sky Sports](https://www.skysports.com/football/news/11679/13106600/pep-guardiola-hits-back-at-roy-keanes-league-two-criticism-of-man-city-striker-erling-haaland)). Keane is the pundit version of the calibration-meeting skeptic, and he is not a fool: he's arguing that all-around contribution *is* part of the job. Guardiola's rebuttal (best striker in the world, judge him on what he's for) is the piece's position, but Keane's view is genuinely held across football.
- **The false-9 school**: a real tactical debate exists over whether Haaland makes City's *system* worse even while making its output better. Before Haaland, Guardiola's false-9 teams dominated midfield and controlled games; with Haaland, City sacrifice control for devastation ([Sky Sports analysis](https://www.skysports.com/football/news/15126/13117892/erling-haaland-how-big-a-problem-is-the-form-of-the-man-city-striker-for-pep-guardiola), [counterpoint](https://medium.com/@jedhill0/attacking-patterns-and-the-false-false-9-yes-haaland-does-make-city-better-3069edbe18f7)). Translation to engineering: an outcome-maximizing individual can degrade system-level properties the dashboard also doesn't capture. The sword cuts both ways.
- **McKinsey** ("Yes, you can measure developer productivity," 2023): the establishment case that refusing to measure is an abdication, that engineering can't remain the only function exempt from accountability, and that imperfect metrics beat none. Most engineering leaders' bosses believe some version of this.
- **Tanya Reilly, "Being Glue"** (https://www.noidea.dog/glue): the most interesting complication, because she's *on Aaron's side* and still breaks the piece's clean binary. The draft splits the world into noisy heroes and quiet finishers. Reilly documents a third figure: the glue worker, highly active, constantly visible in meetings and unblocking others, and *still* not promoted because the activity isn't "technical enough." Glue work is disproportionately done by women. An outcome-only lens punishes them too: unblocking twelve teammates never shows up as *your* outcome. The binary (activity bad, outcomes good) is too clean.

## Cases where the premise failed

- **The quiet coaster is real.** Every experienced manager has had an engineer whose flat dashboard meant exactly what it appeared to mean. The premise, read naively ("assume your quiet people are Haalands"), fails in every such case. The post cannot ask managers to *assume* quiet excellence; it can only ask them to *investigate* before concluding.
- **The hero is often innocent.** The draft's sharpest line ("heroes are firefighting the consequences of their own creations") overclaims. Repenning & Sterman's actual finding is that firefighting is a *system* property: organizations underinvest in capability, crises follow, and whoever's competent gets pulled in. Plenty of heroes are firefighting code they inherited. And Aaron's own Deming anchor from the Batman post ("a bad system will beat a good person every time") argues directly against blaming the individual hero. The piece contradicts its own systems-thinking lineage if it moralizes about heroes instead of indicting the reward system that manufactures them.
- **Haaland himself got benched.** During City's 2024 wobble the flat-general-play critique had real bite; when the system around him faltered, his invisibility outside the box stopped looking like efficiency and started looking like absence. Quiet finishers are only safe inside healthy systems. In a struggling org, the quiet engineer is the first one on the layoff list, and "trust me, I prevent invisible fires" does not survive a RIF spreadsheet.

## Where this leaves the piece

The premise survives, but only after three sharpenings. First, the target must be the *system* that can only perceive activity, not the heroes, who are mostly good people responding to the incentives in front of them (this keeps the piece consistent with the Batman post's Deming lineage). Second, the "no goals column" objection gets answered rather than conceded (Aaron's call, 2026-07-09): software *does* have a goals column — the business metrics. Revenue, uptime, cost, churn, conversion: the scoreboard exists. What football gets for free is *attribution* — a goal arrives with the scorer's name already on it, while business outcomes arrive unattributed. So the manager's counter-move to "we can't measure outcomes" is: yes we can, we just can't skip the work of mapping engineering effort to them. That mapping is the leadership job. Activity metrics are what filling the attribution gap with whatever's easiest to count looks like. This turns the piece's hardest objection into its central prescription. Third, the prescription cannot be "assume your quiet engineers are Haalands"; it has to be "do the attribution work — learn to tell walking-with-purpose from walking away," which is a harder and more honest ask. The Reilly complication (glue work) is worth one honest paragraph, not a footnote: it proves the failure isn't activity-vs-outcomes but *legibility-vs-value*, and that framing is sharper than the draft's current binary.
