# Audience

**ICM Layer**: L0 (identity). Who Aaron writes for. Almost never changes.

## Who reads this blog

Aaron writes for two audiences simultaneously, in the same paragraph:

1. **Smart, experienced colleagues**: peers in tech, engineering, leadership. Mid-career and beyond. They've seen patterns, survived cycles, made the mistakes. They want signal, not setup.

2. **Younger folks coming up**: students, early-career engineers, people entering management for the first time. They want mentoring — but as people, not as subordinates.

## The non-negotiable: no hierarchy

Aaron is **not** writing down to anyone. The blog isn't a lecture, and it isn't an explainer. It's the kind of conversation a senior engineer would have with both a peer and a junior at the same coffee table — naturally pitched so both feel respected.

This rules out specific failure modes:

| Avoid | Why |
|---|---|
| "As you may know..." | Patronizing to peers, gatekeeping to students. |
| "Let me explain why..." | Replace the throat-clearing with the explanation. |
| Over-defining basic terms | Insulting to the experienced reader. |
| Insider jargon without context | Excludes the student. |
| "10 simple steps" formulas | Implies reader can't think. |
| "If you're not familiar with..." | Branches the audience instead of meeting them. |

## The test (apply per paragraph)

> Would a smart 25-year-old who's never managed feel **respected** here, AND would a 20-year veteran feel **not insulted**?

Both must be **yes**. If only one is yes, the paragraph is talking to half the audience and ignoring the other.

## What this implies for content choices

- **Assume competence**. Define only what is genuinely specialized, and define it inline rather than in a glossary.
- **Concrete examples > abstractions**. A specific incident from Aaron's career carries more weight than a generic principle. Both audiences anchor to it.
- **Acknowledge mentoring without performing it**. "Here's something I wish I'd known earlier" lands. "Let me teach you something" doesn't.
- **No "obvious" disclaimers**. Statements like "this should be obvious but..." talk down to peers and exclude juniors who didn't find it obvious.
- **Welcome disagreement**. The audience includes people who'll push back. Posts should leave room for that, not foreclose it.

## Voice consequences

This audience constraint is the reason for several specific voice choices in `voice-and-tone.md`:

- First-person professional perspective ("I've seen...") — places Aaron as a peer narrating, not an authority lecturing.
- Ending with questions — invites the reader into the conversation rather than pronouncing.
- Acknowledging emotional/human dimensions — neither audience is a pure technical reader; both are people.
- Trusting the reader's intelligence — the through-line constraint behind all the others.
